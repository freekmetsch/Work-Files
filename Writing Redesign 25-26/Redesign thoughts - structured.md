## 3. Writing 1 — Timed Test

### What to assess
- **Task response:** Have students written something that matches what was asked? Are they writing intentionally?
- **Grammar and diction:** Appropriate for B1/B2 level.
- **Coherent structure:** Can the student produce a piece that doesn't go all over the place? But because they write two short texts under time pressure, expectations for structural depth should be modest — this is a **tertiary focus**.
- **Genre awareness:** Important but not highly weighted at B1/B2. CEFR only makes genre awareness explicitly prominent at C1. So it matters, but is not a primary focus.

### Grammar at B1/B2
- The recording identifies a specific gap: it's hard to determine what constitutes a B1/B2 grammar error vs. a C1/C2 error.
- Wants concrete examples of what is and isn't acceptable at this level.
- Requests: look up CEFR guidelines and Cambridge recommendations for B1/B2 grammar expectations.

### Text types and prompts
- Cambridge uses an essay + a choice of text type.
- Decisions needed on what kinds of prompts and what kinds of source material (if any) students base their writing on.
- Text types must be clear enough so students understand the expected formality and diction.

### Word count
- Approximately 400 words total for both texts combined.

## 4. Writing 2 — Creative Mimicry

### What students do
- Read a published literary work.
- Write a continuation or missing scene imitating the original author's voice.
- Present and defend their work in a P+Q&A.

### What students need to demonstrate
- **Style identification and imitation:** Describe the author's style, explain how they imitated it.
- **Tone:** Identify and replicate the tone of the source text. Can be done relatively early — students can start identifying tone after reading ~20% of the book.
- **Plot and characterisation:** Important for the extra chapter but best covered later in the course once students have read more.
- **Meaning/purpose of the chapter:** The new chapter should have a point — a message, moral, comparison, or insight, consistent with what the original author does. (Example given: Trevor Noah's memoir, where each story is placed in a broader lens.)

### The book list
- The current book list is **outdated** and needs updating and expanding.
- There are good books that would work well but aren't on the list.
- The recording mentions wanting to share the current book list (not yet provided) and think about how to expand it.

> **Action needed from you:** Share the current book list so it can be reviewed and expanded.

### Presentation & Q&A: clarity of expectations
- The P+Q&A must be designed so the **student is responsible for communicating** the required information — the teacher should not have to fish for it.
- Students get very clear instructions about what information they must cover.
- If a student runs out of time without covering everything, that's their responsibility.
- The teacher can help with clarification questions, but shouldn't be pulling information out.
- The P+Q&A tests **authorship**: even if AI was used, the student must explain their decision-making convincingly. If the examiner has to dig too much and the student can't explain why they did what they did, that's a problem.
- Preparation is expected and allowed (including using AI to prepare).

### Reading pace and course sequencing
- Students can read roughly **50 pages per week** (~2 hours of reading time).
- Between sessions, students spend time on: reading (~2 hrs), a preparation activity (~1 hr), plus the contact hours (~1.5 hrs).
- Because students need to read a significant portion before writing the creative chapter, **the first weeks must focus on theory and writing activities that don't require deep book knowledge**.
- Early sessions can focus on style and tone identification (possible after ~20% of the book).
- Plot and characterisation come later once more has been read.
- Need to be economical with content across 7 sessions (part-time) — can't cover everything.

### Grammar in Writing 2
- There is a **grammar reader** that needs significant updating and possibly trimming.
- Some concepts in the current grammar reader may be old-fashioned.
- May need a digital PDF of a grammar handbook as a grounding source.
- Idea for sessions: a **two-page grammar worksheet** per session, covering about 30 minutes of class time. Students work through theory and do exercises. The worksheet template already exists and is ready to be populated once the grammar reader content is decided.

## 5. Writing 3 — Proposal + Timed Test

### The proposal
- **Audience:** A school board member or section head at the student's internship school.
- **Register:** Semi-formal. Not an academic essay, not casual — a proposal to colleagues.
- **Purpose:** Argumentative. Students propose something (e.g., "We should implement flipped classroom principles in our English lessons") and support it with reasoned arguments.
- **Structure:** Thesis + three points of support.
- **Source selection matters:** Students need to be able to explain why they chose their sources and why those sources are reliable.
- **APA:** Not a formal course goal, but there's an expectation of decent, consistent formatting when writing semi-formally for a professional audience. Source selection quality matters more than perfect APA.

> **Note:** The consolidated design document (v5, 2026-03-06) still lists "Option A: Proposal" and "Option B: Creative piece (Murderless Mystery or Personal Essay)" as student choice for W3 take-home. However, SESSION-CONTEXT.md records a later decision: "W3 Take-Home = Proposal Only — Creative option removed." The voice recording only discusses the proposal for W3, consistent with the removal. The consolidated doc appears not yet updated on this point.

### Presentation & Q&A — what to focus on
This is where the recording expresses the most uncertainty. Key tensions:

**Things that could be assessed in the P+Q&A:**
- Why the student chose this thesis for their specific context
- Why the chosen points of support make sense
- How one body paragraph is built up in detail (topic sentence, source use, development)
- Source selection process — why these sources over others
- Argument selection process — why these arguments
- Topic sentence construction — why this level of specificity
- Genre awareness — how the student adhered to proposal conventions
- Closing thought — how the proposal leaves the reader with a clear impression
- The hook or introduction strategy
- Register choices — keeping yourself out of the text unless appropriate
- Writing strategy decisions more broadly

**The constraint:** Only 15 minutes per student. Can't cover everything.

**Proposed approach:** Focus on a **sample** of the student's work and generalise from that — the goal is to show authorship and decision-making ability, not to walk through the whole proposal top to bottom.

**Relationship to timed test:** The recording suggests a complementary approach — what the timed test already covers doesn't need to be re-tested in the P+Q&A, and vice versa. This could help narrow the P+Q&A focus.

### Timed test — Writing 3
- Cambridge C1 Advanced style.
- Grammar: not taught as a separate topic, but common errors at C1/C2 level should be incorporated (comma splices, run-on sentences, etc.).
- Requests a deep dive into what matters most for demonstrating writing skill in a timed test at C1/C2 level, grounded in CEFR and Cambridge recommendations.

### Session content for Writing 3 (7 sessions, part-time)
Topics to cover across both the proposal and the timed test:

- **Source selection** (for the proposal)
- **Paragraph structure:** One idea per paragraph, practised for both timed and proposal writing
- **Trimming/scoping an idea:** Not too broad, not too narrow — a judgement call
- **Body paragraph development:** Topic sentences, idea development
- **Proposal structure:** Full structure, planning, roadmap thesis
- **Roadmap thesis:** The reader can predict the essay's content from the thesis statement (claim + 3 points)
- **Semi-formal register:** Clarity over abstraction, appropriate word choice, keeping yourself out of it unless relevant, phrasal verbs as a practical activity
- **Genre case studies for the timed test:** Approximately 5–6 genres that Cambridge might include, with brief case studies mapping genre expectations, tone, and formality
- **Common C1/C2 grammar pitfalls** (comma splices, run-on sentences, etc.)

## 6. Design Principles (All Courses)

### Part-time first
- **Design for part-time students first**, then consider full-time.
- Part-time students have 1 session per week; full-time have 2.
- Designing for full-time first would leave part-time students feeling rushed and excluded.
- The course should feel like it's for every student.
- How to handle full-time's extra session is an open question (see Section 8).

### Guided learning experience, not just workshops
- The courses are not 7 standalone workshops. They are a **program** through which students develop writing skills.
- There are expectations **during** sessions and **between** sessions.
- The between-session work should be clear, logical, and slightly repetitive so students develop a writing habit.
- Students coming to class without having done the between-session work should feel that something is off — the session relies on their preparation.

### Predictable session cycle
- The preparation between sessions should be **super predictable** so students get into a habit.
- Each cycle: read/listen to a bit of theory, do a bit of writing, do a bit of research/preparation.
- Students must bring something back to class — used immediately in one of the first session activities (flipped classroom element).
- Not fully flipped: some theory is still covered in-session.
- If the cycle is clear and predictable, higher expectations become reasonable.
- The current frustration: students don't prepare much, but it's not always their fault because expectations aren't always clear.

### Writing as practice, not study
- Writing isn't something you can think your way into being better at — you have to do it.
- Keep instruction minimal; acknowledge that practice is central.
- At the same time, writing is also about **communicating ideas** — so the thinking/planning side matters.
- Writing strategies are the bridge: they help students think about their text at multiple levels (genre, audience, paragraph-level ideas, sentence-level choices, logical connections).
- Students need to think about writing as **a set of activities**, not just generating text.

### Writing strategies (all courses)
Key strategies to build across the courses:
- **Idea generation**
- **Structuring ideas**
- **Trimming ideas** — not too much, not too little for the space
- **Top-down to line-level thinking** — from big picture to sentence editing
- **Logical connections** — something sounding good doesn't mean it's logical

These apply to Writing 1 (improvisational writing), Writing 3 (proposal), and to some degree Writing 2 (creative writing has its own flavour).

## 7. Canvas Design

### Single-page course layout
- Current design: one Canvas page per week (7 modules per course).
- Proposed: **one page for the entire course**.
- Rationale:
  - Students can see the full course structure at a glance.
  - Information is easy to find without digging.
  - Works well in a creative writing course already being taught.
  - Keeps the page minimal — not designed as a self-study guide.

- **No schedule with specific dates** — the page should be block-agnostic and timeless ("set and forget"). No adjustments needed when the course runs in a different block.

## 8. Materials: Theory Reader vs. PowerPoints

### No PowerPoints as a design deliverable
- PowerPoints lead to version confusion and aren't the right backbone for these courses.
- If individual teachers want to make PowerPoints, that's fine — but the course is not designed around them.
- Instead, design **lesson phases/activities** that are clear and followable.

### Theory readers
- Compile a **theory reader** for each course (or shared across courses).
- Writing 1: theory reader (writing strategies, improvisational writing)
- Writing 2: grammar reader + writing theory reader (creative writing theory)
- Writing 3: theory reader (proposal writing, genre awareness, timed test strategies)
- The theory reader is the single source of all theory — no sending students to external websites or articles that can go out of date.
- Standardised format makes it easy to design, easy for colleagues to recognise.
- Lesson cycles can be built around each theory section.

### Extensions/ideas for theory delivery
- The theory reader could be incorporated into a **writing chatbot** — students ask questions about the theory.
- Could use **NotebookLM** to generate podcasts from the theory reader that students listen to as an option.
- These are ideas, not commitments.
