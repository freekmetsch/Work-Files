English Tenses — Overview

Grammar & Basics — Week 7 Catch-Up (Weeks 1 & 2 material)
Present Simple vs. Present Continuous
Present Simple Present Continuous
Use: things true in general, habits, facts
I work at Hogeschool Utrecht.
Water boils at 100°C.
Clue words: every day, usually, always, often, never 		Use: actions happening now, temporary situations
He is getting dressed right now.
I am working at a pizza place this summer.
Clue words: (right) now, at the moment, this week, this summer
Side by side: It rains a lot in this country. (= general truth) vs. It is raining now. Let's stay in. (= happening right now)
Past Simple vs. Present Perfect
Past Simple — finished Present Perfect — not finished / result now
Completed action in the past
I lived in Groningen for 8 years. (= not anymore)
Shakespeare died in 1616.
Clue words: yesterday, ago, last..., in (+ past time), when... 		Action from past until now, OR past action with a result you can still see
I have lived in Utrecht for 6 years. (= still true)
She has lost her key. (= she still doesn't have it)
Clue words: since (+ moment), for (+ duration, still ongoing), yet, already, ever, never
Note: "for" can also appear with Past Simple when the situation has ended — compare the examples above.
Key test: Is there a past time reference that closes off the event (yesterday, last year, in 2003)? → Past Simple. Does the event connect to now — through a result, an experience, or an ongoing situation? → Present Perfect.
Tricky case: She lost her key, but she found it again. (= the situation is now over, so Past Simple — even though it sounds recent)
Past Perfect & Past Continuous — Two Events in the Past
Past Perfect
had + 3rd form (past participle)
e.g. had left, had eaten
Think of the past as a timeline. When you need to "rewind" to something even earlier, use Past Perfect. It's like a flashback in a film.
When the police arrived, the thieves had left the building. (= thieves left before police came)
He arrived after I had left. (= I left first, then he arrived)
Past Continuous
was/were + -ing
Think of it as the background music in a scene — the longer action that was already going on when something else happened.
While I was having a bath, the phone rang. (bath = background, phone = interruption)
At 5 o'clock I was cycling home. (= I was in the middle of cycling)
Future Tenses — Four Ways to Talk About the Future
Form Use Example
will + verb Predictions, promises, and deciding something on the spot I think robots will replace many jobs.
[Phone ringing] I'll get it!
going to + verb 1) Plans already decided
2) Predictions based on what you can see right now		 I'm going to buy a new car. (= decided)
Look at the clouds. It's going to rain. (= I can see evidence)
present continuous Things you've already organised (booked, confirmed) We are flying to Australia tomorrow.
I am seeing my dentist next Tuesday.
present simple Official timetables and schedules (trains, school, events) The train leaves at 15:00.
The autumn break starts on October 20th.
Watch Out! — Common Tense Mistakes by Mother Tongue
If you speak Dutch...
Using "have" when you shouldn't: Dutch says ik heb gedaan for the past, but English often needs Past Simple instead.
I have seen him yesterday.
I saw him yesterday.

"Since" vs "for": Dutch sinds covers both, but English distinguishes them.
I live here since 5 years.
I have lived here for 5 years.
I have lived here since 2021.
If you speak Spanish...
Using -ing too much: Spanish allows progressive forms with more verbs than English does. In English, some verbs (know, want, like, believe) never take -ing.
I am knowing the answer.
I know the answer.

"Going to" vs "will": Spanish ir a feels like "going to," so you might always reach for that. But English also needs "will" — for guesses and on-the-spot decisions.
If you speak Polish...
Missing "have/has": Polish doesn't use a helper word like "have," so you might leave it out.
I already finish my homework.
I have already finished my homework.

Which past tense? Polish uses verb endings to show whether something is done or ongoing. English uses different tenses instead. When in doubt, look for clue words (yesterday, since, while, etc.).

Prepositions — Time, Position & Movement

Grammar & Basics — Week 7 Catch-Up (Week 5 material)
Prepositions of Time: In, On, At
Memory trick — think from big to small: in = big time periods (months, years, seasons) → on = specific days/dates → at = exact times and moments. The smaller the time, the shorter the word.
in
Months, years, seasons, parts of the day, future time spans
in September • in 2024 • in the morning • in one year's time
on
Days and dates
on Monday • on 25 December • on my birthday
at
Specific times, mealtimes, festivals, holidays
at 9 o'clock • at lunch • at Christmas • at the weekend
till / until
Up to a point in time (= until)
My wife drove till 7:00, then I took over. • We waited until the rain stopped.
Exceptions to the trick: Festivals and holidays use "at" even though they last days: at Christmas, at Easter, at the weekend. And parts of the day use "in" (in the morning) — except at night, at noon, and at midnight.
Position vs. Movement
in vs. into
In = position (already there). Into = movement (going there).
I'm in the bathroom. vs. He walked into the bathroom.
Opposite of into = out of: I told him to get out of the bathroom.
to
Movement / direction towards a destination
We drove to Paris. • I go to school every day.
Confusing Pairs
during vs. for
During = when (+ noun). For = how long (+ duration).
I fell asleep during the movie. I slept for half an hour.
off vs. of
Off = away from a surface (opposite of "on"). Of = belonging to or part of (often Dutch van).
The book fell off the table. • He is the head of our department.
as vs. like
As = job/function (the real thing). Like = similarity (not the real thing).
He works as a clown. (= he IS a clown) • He acted like a clown. (= he is NOT a clown)
before vs. in front of
Before = usually time. In front of = position in space.
Shall we meet before 7:00? • I'll wait in front of the restaurant.
in time vs. on time
In time = early enough. On time = at the scheduled time.
I was in time for the announcement. • The show started on time.
Watch Out! — Common Preposition Mistakes by Mother Tongue
If you speak Dutch...
"good in" → good at:
I'm not good in English.
I'm not good at English.

"with the train" → by train:
I go to school with the train.
I go to school by train.

"behind the computer" → at:
I was sitting behind the computer.
I was sitting at the computer.

"since" vs "for": see Tenses theory sheet — same rule applies here as a preposition choice.
If you speak Spanish...
"en" does too much: Spanish en = in, on, at. English distinguishes between them.
I'm en → in school. → I'm at school. (= the place)
I'm en → on the classroom. → I'm in the classroom. (= inside)
The book is en → in the table. → The book is on the table. (= surface)

Wrong preposition after verbs: Spanish para/por often translates as "for," but in English the preposition after verbs is often different.
I'm good for maths. → I'm good at maths.
If you speak Polish...
Forgetting the preposition: Polish changes the word ending instead of adding a small word. In English, you need to add the preposition.
I go school every day.
I go to school every day.

"na" ≠ "on": Polish na sometimes = "at" or "in" in English.
I'm on the university.
I'm at the university.
