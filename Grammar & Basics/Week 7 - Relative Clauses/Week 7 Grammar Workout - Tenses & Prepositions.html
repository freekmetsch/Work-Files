English Tenses — Overview

Grammar & Basics — Week 7 Catch-Up (Weeks 1 & 2 material)
Present Simple vs. Present Continuous
Present Simple Present Continuous
Use: things true in general, habits, facts
I work at Hogeschool Utrecht.
Water boils at 100°C.
Signal words: every day, usually, always, often, never 		Use: actions happening now, temporary situations
He is getting dressed right now.
I am working at a pizza place this summer.
Signal words: (right) now, at the moment, look!, today, this week
Side by side: It rains a lot in this country. (= general truth) vs. It is raining now. Let's stay in. (= happening right now)
Past Simple vs. Present Perfect
Past Simple — finished Present Perfect — not finished / result now
Completed action in the past
I lived in Groningen for 8 years. (= not anymore)
Shakespeare died in 1616.
Signal words: yesterday, ago, last..., in (+ past time), when... 		Action from past until now, OR past action with result now
I have lived in Utrecht for 6 years. (= still true)
She has lost her key. (= she still doesn't have it)
Signal words: since (+ moment), for (+ duration), yet, already, ever, never
Key test: Is it finished and over? → Past Simple. Is it still true now or does it have a result now? → Present Perfect.
But: She lost her key, but she found it again. (= over, so Past Simple even though it sounds recent)
Past Perfect & Past Continuous — Two Events in the Past
Past Perfect
had + past participle
One event happened before another. The earlier event uses Past Perfect.
He arrived after I had left. (= I left first, then he arrived)
When the police arrived, the thieves had left the building. (= thieves left before police came)
Past Continuous
was/were + -ing
A longer background action interrupted by a shorter one. Or two things happening at the same time.
While I was having a bath, the phone rang.
At 5 o'clock I was cycling home. (= in the middle of the action)
Future Tenses — Four Ways to Talk About the Future
Form Use Example
will + verb Predictions, spontaneous decisions I will travel to New York some day.
[Phone ringing] I'll get it!
going to + verb Plans/intentions, signs of something happening I'm going to buy a new car.
Look at the clouds. It's going to rain.
present continuous Arrangements already made We are flying to Australia tomorrow.
I am seeing my dentist next Tuesday.
present simple Fixed timetables/schedules The train leaves at 15:00.
The autumn break starts on October 20th.
Watch Out! — Common Tense Mistakes by Language Background
If you speak Dutch...
Present perfect overuse: Dutch uses the present perfect (ik heb gedaan) where English uses Past Simple.
I have seen him yesterday.
I saw him yesterday.

"Since" vs "for": Dutch sinds covers both, but English distinguishes them.
I live here since 5 years.
I have lived here for 5 years.
I have lived here since 2021.
If you speak Spanish...
Present continuous overuse: Spanish estoy haciendo is used more broadly than English present continuous.
I am knowing the answer.
I know the answer.

"Going to" vs "will": Spanish ir a maps onto "going to," but English also needs "will" for predictions and spontaneous decisions.
If you speak Polish...
No articles + missing auxiliaries: Polish has no "have/has" auxiliary, so present perfect feels unnatural.
I already finish my homework.
I have already finished my homework.

Aspect confusion: Polish aspect (perfective/imperfective) doesn't map directly onto English tense distinctions. Use signal words and context to choose.
Theory Sheet

Prepositions — Time, Position & Movement

Grammar & Basics — Week 7 Catch-Up (Week 5 material)
Prepositions of Time: In, On, At
in
Months, years, seasons, parts of the day, future time spans
in September • in 2024 • in the morning • in one year's time
on
Days and dates
on Monday • on 25 December • on my birthday
at
Specific times, mealtimes, festivals
at 9 o'clock • at lunch • at Christmas • at the weekend
Position vs. Movement
in vs. into
In = position (already there). Into = movement (going there).
I'm in the bathroom. vs. He walked into the bathroom.
Opposite of into = out of: I told him to get out of the bathroom.
to vs. till
To = movement/direction. Till = time (= until).
We drove to Paris. vs. My wife drove till 7:00, then I took over.
Confusing Pairs
during vs. for
During = when (+ noun). For = how long (+ duration).
I fell asleep during the movie. I slept for half an hour.
off vs. of
Off = opposite of "on." Of = all other cases (usually Dutch van).
The book fell off the table. • He is the head of our department.
as vs. like
As = job/function (the real thing). Like = similarity (not the real thing).
He works as a clown. (= he IS a clown) • He acted like a clown. (= he is NOT a clown)
before vs. in front of
Before = time. In front of = position.
Shall we meet before 7:00? • I'll wait in front of the restaurant.
in time vs. on time
In time = early enough. On time = at the scheduled time.
I was in time for the announcement. • The show started on time.
Watch Out! — Common Preposition Mistakes by Language Background
If you speak Dutch...
"good in" → good at:
I'm not good in English.
I'm not good at English.

"with the train" → by train:
I go to school with the train.
I go to school by train.

"behind the computer" → at:
I was sitting behind the computer.
I was sitting at the computer.

"since" vs "for": Dutch sinds covers both.
He has worked here since two months.
He has worked here for two months.
If you speak Spanish...
"en" does too much: Spanish en = in, on, at. English distinguishes between them.
I'm at school. (= the place)
I'm in the classroom. (= inside)
The book is on the table. (= surface)

"para" vs "por" ≠ for: English "for" is simpler, but the preposition after verbs often differs from Spanish.
I'm good at maths. (not "for")
If you speak Polish...
Cases instead of prepositions: Polish uses case endings where English uses prepositions. You may forget the preposition entirely.
I go school every day.
I go to school every day.

"na" ≠ "on": Polish na sometimes = "at" or "in" in English.
I'm on the university.
I'm at the university.
