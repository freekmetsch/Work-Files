Theory Sheet

Linking Words & Relative Clauses

Although / Though / Even Though vs. Despite / In Spite Of

Both express a contrast — something happened even though you wouldn't expect it. But they work differently in how you build the sentence.

although / even though
Followed by a sentence with a subject + verb.
Although it rained a lot, we enjoyed our holiday.
Even though she was tired, she couldn't sleep.
Although the bad weather, we went out. — wrong: no verb after "although"
despite / in spite of
Followed by a noun or -ing form (NOT a subject + verb).
Despite the rain, we enjoyed our holiday.
In spite of being very tired, she couldn't sleep.
Despite she was tired, she continued. — wrong: clause after "despite"
Quick test: Look at what follows. Is there a subject + verb (e.g., "she was tired")? → use although / even though. Is there just a noun or -ing form (e.g., "the rain" / "being tired")? → use despite / in spite of.
As vs. Like
like + noun
Means "similar to" — a comparison (not the real thing).
He acted like a clown in class today. (= he is NOT a clown)
That house looks like a palace.
as + noun
Means "in the role of" — the actual function or job.
He performs as a clown at children's parties. (= he IS a clown)
She works as a nurse at the hospital.
as + subject + verb
Means "in the same way as" when followed by a sentence.
You should do it as I showed you.
Relative Clauses — Who, Which, That
who
Refers to people. The student who sits next to me is from Germany.
which
Refers to things. The book which I borrowed was very helpful.
that
Can replace who or which — but ONLY when the information is essential to identify who/what you mean (see "defining" below).
Defining — the information tells you which person or thing you mean. It is essential. No commas. That is OK.
The woman who/that lives next door is a doctor. — Which woman? The one next door.

Non-defining — the information is extra. You already know who/what is meant. Use commas. That is NOT OK.
My neighbour, who has a lot of dogs, is a great guy. — We already know which neighbour.
My neighbour, that has a lot of dogs, is a great guy. — wrong: no "that" with commas

"Which" for a whole idea (NOT "what" or "that"):
He failed the exam, which surprised everyone.
He failed the exam, what surprised everyone. — wrong
He failed the exam, that surprised everyone. — wrong
Watch Out! — Common Mistakes by Language Background
If you speak Dutch...
hoewel / ondanks: Dutch has the same split, but many Dutch speakers mix them up in English.
Although the rain, we went out.
Despite the rain, we went out.
Although it rained, we went out.

"wat" ≠ "what": Dutch "wat" = English which (not "what") when referring back to a whole idea.
He quit his job, what surprised me.
He quit his job, which surprised me.

"als" does too much: Dutch als = as, like, and if. English keeps these separate.
She works as a nurse. (= her job)
He acts like a child. (= not really a child)
If you speak Spanish...
"aunque" covers everything: English splits this into although (+ sentence) and despite (+ noun / -ing). Since Spanish uses aunque for both, you may pick the wrong English word.
Although it was raining... (sentence after "although")
Despite the rain... (noun after "despite")
Despite it was raining... Although the rain...

"que" is not always "that": In Spanish, que works for everything. English splits it: who (people), which (things).
My teacher, that is very kind, helped me.
My teacher, who is very kind, helped me.

"como" splits into two:
He works as a waiter. (= his job)
He eats like a horse. (= comparison)
If you speak Polish...
chociaż / mimo że: The split exists in Polish too, but despite in English is simpler — just add a noun or -ing form.
Despite she was late, we waited.
Despite being late, we waited.
Although she was late, we waited.

który/która/które → who/which: English is simpler (no gender), but has a rule Polish doesn't: no that between commas.
Warsaw, that is the capital, is large.
Warsaw, which is the capital, is large.

jak / jako: Polish jak covers both "as" and "like."
He works as a chef. (= his job)
He cooks like a chef. (= not really a chef)

Conditionals — Types 0, 1, 2 & 3

Conditional Types at a Glance
Type Use If-part Result part Example
Type 0 General truths, facts if + present simple present simple If you heat water to 100°C, it boils.
If you press this button, the door opens.
Type 1 Real future possibility if + present simple will + verb If it rains tomorrow, I will stay home.
If you study hard, you will pass.
Type 2 Imaginary present / future if + past simple would + verb If I had more time, I would travel more.
If I were rich, I would buy a boat.
Type 3 Imaginary past (too late now) if + past perfect
(had + done/gone/been...)		 would have + past form
(e.g., gone, studied, been)		 If she had studied harder, she would have passed.
If I had known, I would have helped.
Key Rules
No "will" in the if-part
If it rains tomorrow, we will cancel.
If it will rain tomorrow, we will cancel.
Type 2: use "were," not "was"
If I were you, I would accept the offer. — standard / formal
"If I was you..." is common in spoken English but "were" is preferred in writing.
No "would" in the if-part
If I had known, I would have come. (Type 3)
If I would have known, I would have come.
Quick Comparison — Same Situation, Different Types
Type 1 (real)
If she studies hard, she will pass the exam. — It's possible and likely.
Type 2 (imaginary)
If she studied hard, she would pass the exam. — She's not studying hard (but imagine if she did).
If my grandma had wheels, she would be a bike. — Yes, that's a Type 2 conditional!
Type 3 (past, too late)
If she had studied hard, she would have passed the exam. — She didn't study. The exam is over.
Mixed Conditionals

Sometimes the if-clause and result clause refer to different time frames.

Past → Present
(Type 3 if + Type 2 result)
If I had accepted that job, I would be living in London now.
The past decision affects the present situation.
Present → Past
(Type 2 if + Type 3 result)
If I spoke French, I would have applied for that job last month.
Something that is always true about you affected a past opportunity.
Watch Out! — Common Mistakes by Language Background
If you speak Dutch...
Don't put "zou" in the if-part:
If I would win the lottery, I would buy a house.
If I won the lottery, I would buy a house.

Type 2 and 3 feel the same: Dutch uses similar forms for both.
Now: If I had more money, I would travel.
Back then: If I had had more money, I would have travelled.
Ask yourself: am I imagining now, or regretting the past?
If you speak Spanish...
Don't translate the subjunctive directly: Focus on the English pattern.
Type 2: If I had time... (past simple)
Type 3: If I had had time... (past perfect)

"would had" does not exist:
If she had called, I would had come.
If she had called, I would have come.

No "will" in the if-part:
If it will rain, we will stay inside.
If it rains, we will stay inside.
If you speak Polish...
"gdyby" covers Type 2 AND 3: English forces you to choose — this is a new distinction.
Now: If I knew the answer... (past simple)
Back then: If I had known the answer... (past perfect)
Ask: am I imagining now, or regretting the past?

Word order is stricter:
If I had a car, I would drive to work.
I would drive to work if I had a car.
A car if I had, to work I would drive.
